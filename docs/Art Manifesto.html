Artist Manifesto Draft
I am a researcher of form and a translator of the internal landscape. My work is not a product; it is a physical manifestation of a decades-long inquiry into biology, behavior, and the queer, ‘wild’ self. I reject the pressure of production for the depth of the study. My value lies at the intersection of my PhD intellect and my artist hands. I do not merely sell objects; I place artifacts with those who resonate with the narrative. I honor the slow burn, the five-foot risks, and the mental space that belongs entirely to me. I am the Primary Investigator of my own joy.

Books: The Intellectual Bridge

  • “The Age of Insight” by Eric Kandel: Written by a Nobel Prize-winning neuroscientist, this explores the dialogue between brain science and art (specifically Viennese portraits). It will validate why your PhD and your sculptures are actually the same project.
  • “How to Do Nothing: Resisting the Attention Economy” by Jenny Odell: Odell is an artist who writes brilliantly about resisting the “productivity” trap. It’s a perfect read for someone who wants to make art without being consumed by “marketing.”
  • “Wintering: The Power of Rest and Retreat in Difficult Times” by Katherine May: (No relation to your ceramics name, just a great coincidence!) This is a beautiful look at the “fallow” periods of life—perfect for navigating the transitions of perimenopause and career shifts.
  • “The Unknown Craftsman” by Soetsu Yanagi: A classic on the philosophy of “Mingei” (folk art). It discusses the beauty of objects and might help you reconcile the “functional” (mugs) with the “sculptural” (5-foot pieces).

Podcasts & Blogs: The “Wild Self” & Strategy

  • “The Great Women Artists” Podcast: Hosted by Katy Hessel, this dives into the lives of artists who often found their footing later in life or worked outside the “mainstream” gallery system.
  • “Design Matters” with Debbie Millman: Look for episodes with older artists or those who pivoted from “high-level” careers into creative ones.
  • Hyperallergic (Blog/Journal): For when you want to engage with art that is queer, political, and intellectually “weird.” It avoids the fluff of the “lifestyle” art world.
  • The Marginalian (formerly Brain Pickings) by Maria Popova: A masterclass in “thinking about thinking.” Her essays on the intersection of science and poetry are food for your “writer” soul.

Scientific Context: The “Change”

  • “The Menopause Manifesto” by Dr. Jen Gunter: If you haven’t read it, do. As a biologist, you’ll appreciate her “no-nonsense, evidence-based” approach to what is happening to your brain and body right now. It frames this era as a “renovation” rather than a “decline.”

About Lisa May (Revised)

From Synapses to Silica: Mapping the Social Landscape

For twenty years, my work focused on the invisible mechanics of connection. As a social neuroscientist with a PhD focused on motivation and decision-making, I spent my career dissecting how biological systems implement social behavior. I was a researcher of the “we”—mapping how individuals synchronize, cooperate, and find meaning within a group.

Now, I build that social architecture in clay.

My transition from the lab to the kiln wasn’t a departure; it was a shift in medium. I’ve traded the abstract study of the brain for the tactile reality of ceramics, but the inquiry remains: How do we inhabit our social worlds?

The Work: Social Specially and Narrative Artifacts

My practice is an ethnographic study of non-human social worlds, translated into 5-foot ceramic forms and storytelling objects.

  • The Humpback Whales: These pieces are studies in deep-time sociality. To me, a whale is a monument to collective memory, migration, and the “slow pulse” of communal existence. They represent a social intelligence that operates on a scale humans are only beginning to grasp.
  • The Crows: I am captivated by corvid intelligence—their facial recognition, their “funerals,” and their agile, observational social fabric. My crows are the “street-smart” observers of the studio, representing the sharp, reactive, and highly communicative side of social neuroscience.
  • The Narrative Objects: I view my functional work (mugs and plates) as “thought-containers.” They are etched with the internal monologues that occur when we are alone, yet still profoundly shaped by our social connections.

The Philosophy

I live at the intersection of colorful glasses, queer identity, and complex data. My studio is my laboratory, where I explore the friction between my scientific training as a Social Neuroscientist and my “wild self.”

I am not interested in production; I am interested in intellectual rigor and artistic discovery. Every piece I fire is an invitation to the viewer to step out of the “optimized” world and into a more interesting, idiosyncratic mental space.

“Here, think my thoughts.”

The Field Note Framework: Decoupling the Inquiry from the Artifact

1. The Primary Work is the Inquiry My true creative output is the intellectual synthesis of social neuroscience, queer identity, and biological form. The writing is not “marketing”—it is the documentation of a formal study. Whether I am observing the gular pouch of a crow or the collective memory of a whale pod, the thought is the first product.

2. The Ceramics are “Specimens,” Not Products The mugs, plates, and 5-foot sculptures are physical artifacts resulting from the inquiry. They are the “data points” I’ve pulled from the kiln. Some data points are clean (a perfect glaze); some are messy (a kiln fail). But a messy data point does not invalidate the research.

3. Protect the “Mental Space” from the “Kiln Gods” By publishing my “Field Notes” independently of the final objects, I protect my intellectual labor from the volatility of the ceramic process. I allow myself to be a storyteller and a scientist first, and a manufacturer second. This removes the “transactional anxiety” and ensures that my “wild self” always has a voice, even when the clay misbehaves.

4. The Value Proposition High-value collectors aren’t just buying a 5-foot sculpture; they are buying the five years of documented “Field Notes” that led to it. The writing builds the provenance of depth that justifies the transition from “maker” to “master.”

The Mantra: The thought is the study; the clay is the evidence. I am the Primary Investigator, and the research stands on its own.
